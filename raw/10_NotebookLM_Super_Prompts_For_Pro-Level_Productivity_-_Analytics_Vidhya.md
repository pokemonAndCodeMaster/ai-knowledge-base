The “Essential Questions” Prompt\n\nIf you want clarity fast from a dense pack of material like PDFs, notes, articles, or research papers, this should be your go-to prompt. Instead of long summaries, it forces NotebookLM to identify the core questions that truly matter. The idea is to convey the core information in the form of questions that build a mental framework around the topic. Once you understand them, everything else falls into place.\n\nBest for:\n\nAmong the top prompts, this one is especially powerful for studying, lecture preparation, or understanding unfamiliar and complex topics on NotebookLM.\n\nPrompt:\n\nGo through all my uploaded materials and identify the 5 most important questions\n\nthat someone must be able to answer to truly understand this topic.\n\nThese questions should collectively capture the core meaning of the sources.\n\nWhile forming the questions, consider:\n\nThe main topics and definitions\n\nThe key ideas that are emphasized repeatedly\n\nHow different concepts relate to each other\n\nWhere and how the ideas are applied in real life\n\nOutput:\n\n2. The “Interesting & Surprising Insights” Prompt\n\nWe tend to memorise better when the information being consumed sparks our interest. This is the mental hack being used in the prompt here. The idea behind it is to surprise ourselves with interesting tidbits around the topic, which help instantly memorise a core detail. The prompt is meant to help you uncover surprising facts, non-obvious insights, and quotable moments buried inside your sources. Hot tip: with light steering, you can even control the kind of surprises you want NotebookLM to focus on.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis prompt is extremely useful for writers, researchers, and content creators looking for hooks, contrarian ideas, or fresh perspectives.\n\nPrompt:\n\nI am researching and writing about [TOPIC].\n\nFrom all uploaded sources, identify the most surprising, unexpected,\n\nor especially interesting facts, ideas, or insights related to this topic.\n\nFor each insight:\n\nExplain why it is surprising or noteworthy\n\nInclude direct quotes from the sources to support it\n\nFocus specifically on [SPECIFIC ASPECT of the topic],\n\nand avoid emphasizing [OTHER ASPECTS] unless absolutely necessary.\n\nOutput:\n\n3. The “Source Gap” Prompt\n\nThis prompt will take your research work to the next level. Instead of repeating the information within your sources, this prompt forces it to act like an auditor. NotebookLM checks “what is missing” within your sources, and where they make assumptions or disagree with each other. This is one of the most brilliant power hacks to know what is unknown.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis NotebookLM prompt is ideal for market research, strategy decks, whitepapers, and future-oriented insights.\n\nPrompt:\n\nReview all the uploaded documents together and identify what is missing, not what is already covered.\n\nI want to uncover the hidden gaps based on current industry standards for [TOPIC], especially in the context of 2026 market realities.\n\nSpecifically:\n\nPoint out critical data, perspectives, or discussions that should be present but are absent\n\nHighlight where the sources make assumptions without clear evidence\n\nClearly map contradictions by showing where Source A disagrees with Source B (use direct quotes)\n\nSuggest 5 precise follow-up research questions I should run in Deep Research Mode to close these gaps\n\nAvoid generic summaries.\n\nBase all contradictions on direct citations.\n\nFocus on what an informed reader would expect but not find in these sources.\n\nOutput:\n\nAlso read: Prompt Engineering: Definition, Examples, Tips and More\n\n4. The Contradictions Prompt\n\nWant to prepare for a presentation in front of your bosses? This prompt will quickly become your best friend. One of the most powerful prompts for NotebookLM today, it forces the AI tool to catch contradictions within your uploaded material. It makes NotebookLM quickly sift through all the material and find all contradicting information, making both sides of the coin clear to you. In doing so, you gain unprecedented clarity and exposure to “the other side” as well.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis prompt is perfect for literature reviews, research memos, investment theses, and any “what should we believe?” situation.\n\nPrompt:\n\nAcross all uploaded papers on [TOPIC], identify the biggest contradictions or conflicting findings.\n\nFor each contradiction:\n\nQuote the specific claim from each side (with citations)\n\nExplain why they might disagree (differences in method, sample, timeframe, definitions, or context)\n\nTell me what evidence would resolve it (what data, experiment, study design, or comparison is needed)\n\nDo not summarise the papers.\n\nOnly focus on meaningful conflicts that change the conclusion or decision.\n\nOutput:\n\n5. The Hidden Connections Prompt\n\nThis prompt is your idea-generator when you're trying to connect dots that aren't obvious. In short, it helps you discover hidden bridges between two concepts. The best part: it still stays grounded, because it forces quotes, flags conflicts, and admits where the sources don't fully support the connection.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis prompt is useful for original writing, research ideation, product strategy, and building new frameworks.\n\nPrompt:\n\nExplore and synthesize the connection, even if abstract, between [TOPIC 1] and [TOPIC 2]\n\nusing all uploaded sources.\n\nFor each relevant source:\n\nQuote the key evidence that relates to either topic\n\nConnect it to other relevant information from the materials\n\nPoint out any conflicting viewpoints or tensions\n\nHighlight interesting or unexpected combinations of ideas\n\nThen produce a clear summary that focuses only on how these two topics connect.\n\nGround every insight in direct quotes.\n\nClearly acknowledge gaps where the sources do not strongly support the connection.\n\nOutput:\n\n6. The Quiz/ Debate Prompt\n\nAn interesting conversation or to-and-fro around a topic is always more captivating than reading a write-up on a piece of paper. This prompt plays on this hack and turns passive reading into active thinking. It forces NotebookLM to frame content as a quiz or a debate to help you test understanding, uncover weak spots, and explore opposing viewpoints. It is easy to figure out how the quiz format can reinforce recall. Similarly, the debate format sharpens critical reasoning and helps you widen your horizon by exposing you to opposing viewpoints.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis prompt is brilliant for revision, group discussions, podcasts, and workshops.\n\nPrompt:\n\nCreate an interactive discussion based on the uploaded sources about [TOPIC].\n\nYou can use either of these formats:\n\nOption 1: Quiz Format\n\nTwo hosts\n\nOne host quizzes the other on [TOPIC]\n\n10 questions total (mix of multiple-choice and True/False)\n\nThe respondent should get some answers wrong\n\nCorrections must include short explanations grounded in the sources\n\nShare the final score at the end\n\nOption 2: Debate Format\n\nTwo hosts with opposing viewpoints on [TOPIC]\n\nHost 1 argues for [POSITION A]\n\nHost 2 argues for [POSITION B]\n\nThey should challenge each other's arguments using evidence from the sources\n\nLet the audience decide which side made the stronger case\n\nBase all questions, answers, and arguments on the uploaded materials.\n\nAvoid generic opinions or unsupported claims.\n\nOutput:\n\nAlso read: 7 Cool Google NotebookLM Features No One Told You\n\n7. The Multilingual Podcast Prompt\n\nNeed to learn a new language, or simply sharpen your vocabulary of it? Try this power hack on NotebookLM. It is meant to give you the information you want, in the language you want it. It forces NotebookLM to stay fully immersed in the language of your choice, rather than slipping back into English. The prompt is also useful when you want to consume or present information in a language you're more comfortable with.\n\nBest for:\n\nIdeal for regional podcasts, vernacular explainers, and language learning through real technical or academic material.\n\nPrompt:\n\nCreate a deep-dive discussion based on the uploaded sources, conducted entirely in [TARGET LANGUAGE].\n\nUse only [TARGET LANGUAGE] throughout the response. Do not use English at any point, except when absolutely necessary to clarify a technical term that cannot be translated cleanly.\n\nKeep the tone natural and conversational, as if this were a real podcast or spoken explanation.\n\nOutput:\n\n8. The Product Manager Prompt\n\nThis one is for the professionals who want to cut through the fluff and get to the point pronto. The prompt is meant to extract important signals and highlight what actually impacts users and execution. The prompt is extremely effective when you're dealing with critical documents and need decisions, not explanations.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis is one of the most useful NotebookLM prompts and is perfect for roadmap planning, strategy reviews, and leadership summaries.\n\nPrompt:\n\nReview the uploaded documents as if you were preparing a decision memo for a senior product review.\n\nIgnore fluff and commentary. Focus only on insights that affect real decisions. Summarise your findings in bullet points under these sections:\n\nUser Evidence: Direct quotes that reveal real user problems, needs, or pain points\n\nFeasibility Checks: Technical, operational, or resource constraints mentioned\n\nBlind Spots: Important information or considerations that are missing from the sources\n\nIf my question or objective is vague, pause and ask me to clarify before making assumptions or drawing conclusions.\n\nOutput:\n\n9. The School Teacher Prompt\n\nIf you truly wish to understand a topic, this prompt is for you. It forces NotebookLM to explain complex material as if teaching a 7th grader. In doing this, it exposes gaps in understanding and simplifies dense ideas without dumbing them down. The best part – it correlates every important point with a real-world scenario or example for further ease of learning.\n\nBest for:\n\nThis prompt is ideal for learning a new topic, revising it, or explaining complex topics to unaware audiences.\n\nPrompt:\n\nExplain the uploaded material as if you were teaching a curious 7th-grade student.\n\nUse simple language and short sentences.\n\nDo not assume prior knowledge.\n\nStructure every response like this:\n\ntl;dr: One clear sentence using very simple words\n\nAnalogy: A real-world example or metaphor to explain the idea\n\nVocabulary List: 3 difficult words, each explained in plain language\n\nIf any section is dense or technical, convert it into a short True/False quiz to make it easier to understand.\n\nOutput:\n\nAlso read: 7 Latest AI Drops by Google Will Make You a Powerhouse at Work\n\n10. The Self-Improvement Prompt\n\nThey say constant self-improvement is the key to success. NotebookLM is now here to help with it, thanks to one simple prompt.
